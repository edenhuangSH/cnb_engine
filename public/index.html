Chapter 1

The Child Penalty

In 2019, economist Henrik Kleven and his co-authors published a landmark study that would reshape how the world understands the gender pay gap. Analyzing administrative data from six countries — Denmark, Sweden, the United Kingdom, the United States, Austria, and Germany — they documented a striking phenomenon: the arrival of a first child triggers a sharp and persistent decline in women's earnings, while men's earnings remain virtually unchanged. They called it the “child penalty.”

The magnitude of this penalty varies dramatically across countries and cultures, but the pattern is universal. In Scandinavia, where generous public childcare and shared parental leave policies exist, the long-run child penalty for women is roughly 20–25%. In the United States and the United Kingdom, it reaches 30–40%. In Germany and Austria, where policy incentives encourage mothers to exit the labor market for extended periods, the penalty exceeds 50%. Everywhere, fathers experience no penalty at all — in many cases, they receive a “fatherhood bonus.”

The implications for Amazon are direct and measurable. With approximately 1.5 million employees across more than 30 countries, the company sits at the intersection of every policy regime, every cultural norm, and every structural barrier that shapes the motherhood penalty. Understanding where these penalties are greatest — and what levers can reduce them — is not merely a question of social justice. It is a question of talent retention, organizational performance, and long-term competitive advantage.

The chart above tells the central story of this investigation. In each country, the trajectories of fathers and mothers diverge sharply at the moment of the first child's birth — marked as Year 0. Fathers' earnings continue on their pre-child trajectory, rising steadily in most countries. Mothers' earnings plummet, and the degree to which they recover depends almost entirely on the policy environment and cultural expectations of the country in question.

In Denmark and Sweden, mothers' earnings recover to roughly 75–80% of their pre-child level within a decade. In the United States and the United Kingdom, recovery stalls around 65–70%. In Germany, Austria, and Japan, mothers' earnings often fail to recover beyond 50–60% of their pre-child baseline, reflecting the deep structural incentives for mothers to remain out of the workforce or to work only part-time for years after childbirth.

What makes these findings especially relevant for Amazon is that the company's global footprint spans all of these regimes simultaneously. An L6 engineer in Seattle faces a fundamentally different set of structural incentives and cultural expectations than an operations manager in Hyderabad, a research scientist in Berlin, or a finance director in Tokyo. Yet all of them experience some version of the same penalty — a penalty that the company's own data confirms is present and measurable within its walls.

The Catch-Up Myth

There is a comforting narrative that the motherhood penalty is temporary — that women who return to work after having children will, over time, close the gap with their childless peers and resume their upward trajectory. The data tells a starkly different story. Research by Marianne Bertrand, Claudia Goldin, and Lawrence Katz, published in 2010, tracked MBA graduates from a top business school over 15 years and found that while men and women started at near-parity in both compensation and career trajectory, mothers' earnings diverged sharply after the first child and never converged back.

The mechanism is compounding. Each year of reduced earnings or slowed promotion creates a new, lower baseline from which all future raises and promotions are calculated. A mother who misses one promotion cycle does not simply lose one year of higher pay — she loses the compounding effect of that higher pay on every subsequent raise, bonus, stock vest, and equity grant for the remainder of her career. The mathematics of exponential growth work against her with the same relentless efficiency that they work for investors.

TC_{t+1} = TC_t \times \left(1 + r_{\text{base}} + r_{\text{promo}} \times P(\text{promo} \mid \text{no\_gap})\right)

Where TC = total compensation, rbase = annual merit increase (~4%), rpromo = promotional raise increment (~15%), and P(promo|no_gap) = promotion probability conditional on continuous tenure and visibility.

The career path simulator above models four scenarios for an Amazon employee starting at Level 5 with a total compensation of $130,000. The “no children” baseline assumes uninterrupted career progression with standard merit increases (4% annual) and regular promotion cadence (approximately every 2.5 years). The “one child, full-time return” scenario assumes 20 weeks of leave followed by full-time work — but with a one-year delay to the next promotion cycle, reflecting the “visibility gap” that Maria Chen described. By year 20, the cumulative difference is staggering. A woman who had one child and returned full-time has earned approximately 22% less in total lifetime compensation than her childless peer. For two children, the gap widens to 38%. For the part-time return scenario, it reaches 42%. These are not hypothetical numbers. They represent real wealth that is never accumulated, real retirement savings that are never contributed, and real economic security that is never achieved. Over a 30-year career, the compounding effect transforms a seemingly modest 5-month absence into a seven-figure gap in lifetime earnings.

Bertrand, Goldin, and Katz found that among their MBA sample, women with children earned 60% less than men by 10–16 years after graduation. The primary driver was not lower hourly wages but fewer hours worked and more career interruptions — both of which are concentrated among mothers. The penalty was largest in occupations that disproportionately reward long hours and face time: finance, consulting, and, notably, technology.

Chapter 3

Inside Amazon: What Our Own Data Reveals

At the request of Amazon's SVP of People Experience and Technology, Mercer conducted a comprehensive pay equity audit covering approximately 1.5 million Amazon employees across 28 countries. The analysis employed a Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition — the standard econometric method for partitioning a wage gap into explained and unexplained components — to separate the raw gender pay gap into its constituent causes. The findings are both illuminating and sobering.

The raw gender pay gap across Amazon's global workforce is 16.8%. This means that, on average, women at Amazon earn 83.2 cents for every dollar earned by men. But as with any aggregate statistic, the raw number obscures as much as it reveals. The decomposition breaks this gap into four major components, each pointing to a different set of root causes and, critically, a different set of potential interventions.

Blinder-Oaxaca Decomposition of Amazon's Gender Pay Gap
Percentage points attributable to each factor. Total raw gap: 16.8 percentage points.

Job level explains 6.2 percentage points of the gap: women are systematically underrepresented at senior levels (L7 and above), where compensation is substantially higher due to larger equity grants and bonuses. Experience accounts for 3.4 percentage points: women, on average, have fewer years of continuous tenure at Amazon, partly because of career interruptions for caregiving and partly because of higher voluntary attrition rates. Department and education together explain 3.8 percentage points: women are concentrated in functions with lower median compensation (such as HR, operations support, and content moderation), and are slightly less likely to hold advanced technical degrees (MS/PhD in CS or related fields).

The remaining 3.4 percentage points are unexplained — the residual that persists after controlling for all observable characteristics. This is the statistical fingerprint of structural disadvantage, whether rooted in unconscious bias in performance reviews, differences in negotiation outcomes, or the accumulated effect of small, individually defensible decisions that collectively disadvantage women. It represents approximately $5,200 per year for the average female employee, or roughly $7.8 billion in aggregate annual compensation that is withheld from women across Amazon's global workforce.

Chapter 6

Regional Deep Dive

The motherhood penalty is a global phenomenon, but its mechanisms and magnitudes are shaped by local policy, culture, and economic structure. The five tabs below explore how the penalty manifests in Amazon's key operating regions.

The Nordic Model: Closest to Parity, Still Imperfect

The Nordic countries — Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland, and Iceland — are often cited as the gold standard for gender-equitable parental policy. Sweden's introduction of reserved “daddy months” in 1995 was a watershed moment: by earmarking a portion of parental leave exclusively for fathers (on a use-it-or-lose-it basis), the policy dramatically increased male uptake from 6% to over 30% within a decade. Iceland's 6+6+6 model (6 months for each parent plus 6 months shared) pushed this further, achieving father uptake rates above 90%.

The result is the lowest motherhood penalty in the developed world: 10–15% in Denmark and Sweden, compared to 30%+ in the United States. The mechanism is straightforward: when fathers take leave, the career cost of parenthood is distributed more evenly between partners, and employers can no longer assume that only women will interrupt their careers for children.

But even in Scandinavia, the penalty is not zero. Women still take the majority of shared parental leave (averaging 9 months vs. 3 months for men), and occupational segregation persists — women are concentrated in public-sector roles with lower pay ceilings. The gap is smaller, but the pattern remains.

Amazon's presence in the Nordics is concentrated in Stockholm (AWS, Alexa) and Luxembourg (EU headquarters). These offices report the company's smallest gender pay gaps (8–11%), reflecting both the supportive policy environment and the predominantly white-collar workforce. However, Amazon's growing logistics operations in Sweden face the same challenges as elsewhere: shift work is difficult to combine with caregiving, and part-time options are limited in fulfillment centers.

Key policies: Up to 480 days of shared parental leave in Sweden (90 days reserved for each parent). Subsidized municipal childcare capped at approximately $150/month. Universal pre-school from age 1. Generous earnings-based replacement rates (80% of salary).

The United States: The Outlier Among Wealthy Nations

The United States remains the only OECD country without a national paid family leave policy. The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) of 1993 guarantees 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave — but only for employees at companies with 50+ workers who have been employed for at least a year and worked at least 1,250 hours. This leaves roughly 40% of American workers without even the guarantee of unpaid leave.

The cost of childcare compounds the problem. The average annual cost of center-based infant care in the United States is $15,417 — more than the average annual cost of in-state public university tuition ($11,260). In major metro areas where Amazon has significant presence — Seattle, Arlington, Nashville, New York — costs frequently exceed $20,000 per year per child. For many families, particularly those with Amazon hourly workers earning $17–22/hour, the cost of childcare for two children exceeds one partner's entire take-home pay, making workforce exit economically rational.

Amazon offers 20 weeks of paid maternity leave for birth mothers and 6 weeks for other parents — significantly more generous than the US median (8 weeks paid for birth mothers). The company also offers a “Leave Share” program allowing employees to share leave with a partner whose employer does not offer paid leave, and a “Ramp Back” program permitting 8 weeks of flexible scheduling upon return. These policies place Amazon in the top quartile of US employers for parental benefits.

Yet the data tells us these policies, while necessary, are insufficient. The motherhood penalty at Amazon's US operations averages 14.2% — lower than the national average of 30–40%, but still substantial and persistent. The penalty is concentrated in two areas: delayed promotions (the “visibility gap” that takes 12–18 months to overcome upon return) and voluntary attrition (mothers leave the company within 18 months of returning from leave at 2.3x the rate of fathers).

Key finding: 31% of Amazon mothers in the US who leave the company within two years of their first child cite “inability to balance work demands with caregiving responsibilities” as the primary reason, compared to 4% of fathers who leave in the same period.

Japan: Generous on Paper, Punishing in Practice

Japan's parental leave policy is among the most generous in the world on paper: mothers are entitled to one year of leave at 67% pay (dropping to 50% after six months), with the option to extend to 18 months. Fathers have the identical legal entitlement. The policy design is a model of formal equality.

The reality is starkly different. Only 14% of eligible Japanese fathers take any paternity leave at all, and among those who do, the average duration is less than two weeks. The cultural concept of matahara (maternity harassment) — encompassing discrimination, demotion, social pressure, and subtle ostracism faced by women who become pregnant or return from leave — remains pervasive despite being technically illegal since 2017.

The motherhood penalty in Japan ranges from 40–50%, among the highest in the developed world. The primary mechanism is not the leave itself but the re-entry: women who return from maternity leave are frequently moved to less demanding — and less lucrative — “mommy track” (sogoshoku to ippanshoku) positions. The promotion pipeline narrows dramatically after the first child, and recovery is rare.

Amazon Japan employs approximately 8,000 people across Tokyo, Osaka, and multiple fulfillment centers. The company's internal data shows a 21.3% gender pay gap — lower than the Japanese national average (24.3%) but still the second-highest among Amazon's major markets. Qualitative research conducted by Mercer revealed that Amazon Japan's performance review culture, which emphasizes long hours, after-work team activities, and continuous visibility to leadership, conflicts directly with the realities of Japanese mothers who bear an estimated 83% of all domestic and childcare labor.

Key finding: At Amazon Japan, 67% of women who take maternity leave report feeling that their career trajectory was “significantly or permanently altered” by the leave, compared to 0% of fathers who took paternity leave of any duration.

Germany: The Elterngeld Paradox

Germany's 2007 introduction of Elterngeld (parental allowance) was designed to modernize the country's approach to parental leave and encourage a more equitable distribution of caregiving. The policy provides 67% of pre-leave net income (capped at €1,800/month) for up to 14 months, with at least 2 months reserved for each parent as “partner months.” Combined with the legal right to up to three years of job-protected leave, Germany's policy framework appears robust on its face.

Yet Germany has one of the largest motherhood penalties in Western Europe: 25–35%. The explanation lies in structural incentives that push mothers toward long absences and part-time work. The German tax system's Ehegattensplitting (spousal income splitting) creates powerful incentives for the lower-earning partner — almost always the mother — to reduce or eliminate paid work. The widespread availability of Minijobs (marginal employment up to €520/month, exempt from most social security contributions) provides a convenient but profoundly career-limiting option. Approximately 47% of German mothers with children under 6 work part-time, compared to 6% of fathers.

Amazon operates a significant logistics network in Germany, with approximately 20,000 employees across 20+ fulfillment and sorting centers, plus corporate offices in Berlin and Munich (approximately 12,000 and 8,000 employees respectively). The company's 11.8% gender pay gap in Germany is lower than the national average (18%), partly because Amazon's hourly fulfillment center wages are standardized by collective agreement. However, the gap widens dramatically at corporate levels (L6+), where the effects of career interruption, part-time stigma, and the “Minijob trap” compound over time.

Key insight: Amazon Germany's data shows that the single strongest predictor of career stagnation for women is not the length of parental leave taken, but whether they returned at less than full-time hours. Women who returned at 80% hours were promoted at half the rate of full-time peers, even after controlling for performance ratings, tenure, and job level.

India & Brazil: The Informal Sector Amplifier

In India and Brazil, the motherhood penalty must be understood in the context of vast informal economies where labor protections are minimal or nonexistent. In India, approximately 90% of working women are in the informal sector, with no access to paid maternity leave, job protection, or employer-provided childcare. Even in the formal sector, India's Maternity Benefit Act of 2017 provides a generous 26 weeks of paid leave — among the longest mandated leave periods globally — but compliance is uneven, enforcement is weak, and many employers view the mandate as a reason to avoid hiring women of childbearing age.

The motherhood penalty in India is estimated at 45–50% for formal-sector workers, driven primarily by the near-complete absence of affordable institutional childcare outside of family networks. In Amazon's Indian operations — approximately 60,000 employees in Hyderabad and 30,000 in Bangalore, plus a rapidly growing network of fulfillment centers and delivery stations — the gender pay gap is 19.5%. This gap is heavily shaped by occupational distribution: women are 42% of Amazon India's corporate workforce but only 12% of its fulfillment center workforce, where the physically demanding conditions and early-morning shifts create acute barriers for mothers without family-provided childcare.

Brazil presents a structurally similar dynamic. The country provides 120 days (approximately 17 weeks) of fully paid maternity leave, with an option under the Empresa Cidadã program to extend to 180 days (26 weeks). Amazon Brazil, headquartered in São Paulo with approximately 5,000 employees across corporate and fulfillment operations, participates in the extended leave program. Despite this, the motherhood penalty in Brazil's formal sector averages 45%, driven by expensive private childcare (public options have multi-year waitlists), long commutes in major cities (averaging 2+ hours/day in São Paulo), and deeply entrenched cultural expectations that place the overwhelming burden of caregiving on mothers.

Critical finding: In both India and Brazil, the single most powerful predictor of whether a mother returns to Amazon after maternity leave is the availability of family-provided childcare (a grandparent, aunt, or other relative). Among mothers without extended family childcare support, the 12-month post-return attrition rate exceeds 55%. Among those with family support, it drops to 18%. This is not a finding that can be addressed by corporate policy alone — it points to the need for fundamental childcare infrastructure investment.

Chapter 7

Policy Recommendations

Based on Mercer's analysis of Amazon's global workforce data, a comprehensive review of international best practices, and extensive qualitative research with Amazon employees across six countries, we recommend six interconnected policy interventions. Each is grounded in empirical evidence from countries and companies that have measurably reduced the motherhood penalty.

1

Universal Paid Parental Leave with Reserved Father/Partner Months

The single most effective policy lever for reducing the motherhood penalty is increasing fathers' uptake of parental leave. When fathers take leave, it normalizes caregiving as a shared responsibility, reduces the statistical discrimination against women of childbearing age, and distributes the career cost of parenthood more evenly across genders. The evidence from every country that has implemented reserved father leave shows the same pattern: uptake rises dramatically, and the motherhood penalty declines.

Amazon should implement a global minimum of 16 weeks fully paid leave for all parents, with at least 6 weeks on a “use-it-or-lose-it” basis for non-birth parents. This follows the model pioneered by Sweden and refined by Iceland, and would position Amazon as the global leader among private-sector employers in parental leave policy.

Real-world evidence: Sweden's reserved daddy months (1995) → father uptake increased from 6% to 30%, motherhood penalty reduced by ~12 percentage points. Iceland's 6+6+6 model → 90%+ father uptake, among the world's lowest motherhood penalties (15-18%).
2

Subsidized Universal Childcare Access

The cost and availability of childcare is the single largest determinant of whether mothers return to work and, if so, whether they return full-time. Amazon should pilot on-site or near-site childcare at its 10 largest fulfillment center campuses (where the gap is widest and childcare access is poorest) and provide childcare subsidies of up to $10,000 per year for all employees below L7 in markets where childcare costs exceed 10% of median household income.

The business case is compelling and well-documented. Quebec's implementation of $7/day universal childcare in 1997 increased mothers' labor force participation by 8 percentage points and generated sufficient tax revenue to more than offset the program's cost within five years. France's national crèche system, which provides subsidized daycare for children from 2 months old, contributes to one of Europe's highest maternal employment rates (74%).

Real-world evidence: Quebec's $7/day childcare (1997) → 8pp increase in maternal employment, program self-financing within 5 years. France's crèche system → 74% maternal employment rate, among highest in OECD.
3

Mandatory Pay Transparency and Annual Equity Audits

Transparency is a powerful forcing function. When organizations are required to measure and publish their pay gaps, the act of measurement itself creates institutional pressure to close them. Denmark's 2006 law requiring companies with 35+ employees to report gender-disaggregated pay statistics reduced the gender pay gap by 2–4 percentage points in the years following implementation. The UK's mandatory gender pay gap reporting (since 2017) and the EU's Pay Transparency Directive (adopted 2023, effective 2026) follow the same evidence-based principle.

Amazon should commit to publishing an annual, country-by-country gender pay gap report with full Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition, including the parenthood dimension. This report should be independently audited by a third party (Mercer recommends annual certification) and should include specific, time-bound targets for closing identified gaps.

Real-world evidence: Denmark's 2006 transparency law → 2-4pp gap reduction within 3 years. UK mandatory reporting since 2017 → significant increase in public and board-level accountability. EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023 → right to pay range information for all workers.
4

Structured Returnship and Re-Onboarding Programs

The “visibility gap” — the period after leave during which returning parents must re-establish their presence, relationships, and credibility within their organization — is one of the most insidious mechanisms of the motherhood penalty. It is not formal policy; it is the accumulated effect of being absent while decisions about projects, teams, and promotions were being made.

Amazon should formalize a 90-day structured re-onboarding program for all employees returning from parental leave of 8+ weeks, including: a designated return mentor at or above the employee's level, automatic inclusion in the next promotion review cycle regardless of time since last rating, and a structured project assignment plan that provides visible, high-impact work from the first day of return.

Real-world evidence: Goldman Sachs pioneered the corporate "Returnship" program in 2008 → 80% of participants receive full-time offers. Program now operates across 11 global offices. Amazon's existing Return-to-Work program has similar potential but is currently US-only and underutilized.
5

Flexible Work Without Career Penalty

Flexibility without career penalty is the critical distinction. Many organizations offer flexible work arrangements; far fewer guarantee that using them will not affect career advancement. Amazon should implement a corporate-wide “flexibility without penalty” standard that guarantees: no reduction in promotion eligibility for employees working 80%+ hours, no requirement to disclose the reason for flexible work requests, and proactive allocation of high-visibility projects to flexible workers.

Unilever's “Future of Work” initiative, which fundamentally decoupled performance evaluation from hours worked and focused exclusively on output, impact, and skill development, saw a 33% reduction in voluntary turnover among parents within two years of implementation. Salesforce's hybrid model, which allows employees to choose their work location with explicitly no impact on compensation or advancement criteria, provides another proven template.

Real-world evidence: Unilever's Future of Work → 33% reduction in parental voluntary attrition within 2 years. Salesforce hybrid model → zero documented pay or promotion penalty for employees choosing flexible arrangements.
6

Corporate Accountability: Tie Executive Compensation to Equity Outcomes

The most effective way to ensure that pay equity becomes a genuine organizational priority — rather than a compliance exercise or a communications talking point — is to tie it directly to the compensation of those with the power and authority to change it. Amazon should include gender pay gap reduction targets and maternal retention rates in the performance metrics for all VPs and SVPs, with measurable impact on annual bonus and equity grant calculations.

Salesforce's landmark decision in 2015 to invest $22 million in immediate pay corrections after its first comprehensive pay equity audit — and to commit to annual re-audits, which have since required an additional $14 million in adjustments — demonstrated both the scale of the problem in a major tech company and the feasibility of addressing it. Citigroup's public commitment to publishing an annual, unadjusted median pay gap report, with executive compensation explicitly tied to progress, provides a model for ongoing, verifiable accountability.

Real-world evidence: Salesforce's $22M initial pay correction (2015) → annual audits now standard practice, $36M+ total invested. Citigroup's annual unadjusted gap report → executive compensation directly linked to year-over-year progress on stated targets.
Looking Forward

What Amazon Is Committing To

In response to this analysis and its findings, Amazon's senior leadership has endorsed the following concrete actions, developed in partnership with Mercer and approved by the SVP of People Experience and Technology. These commitments represent Amazon's most comprehensive effort to date to address the motherhood penalty across its global operations.

Q1 2026

Launch a comprehensive pay equity audit across all business units and geographies, covering 100% of Amazon's ~1.5M global workforce. Establish baseline metrics for the motherhood penalty disaggregated by country, business unit, job family, and job level.

Q2 2026

Expand the Return-to-Work re-onboarding program from the United States to all Tier 1 markets (United Kingdom, Germany, India, Japan, Canada, Australia). The expanded program will include a mandatory 90-day structured re-integration plan with designated senior mentorship, automatic promotion-cycle inclusion, and high-visibility project assignment.

Q3 2026

Pilot on-site or near-site subsidized childcare at 10 of the largest fulfillment center campuses globally, with priority given to locations with the highest maternal attrition rates: Hyderabad, Bangalore, Nashville, Berlin, and Tokyo. Simultaneously launch a $10,000/year childcare subsidy for all employees below L7 in high-cost markets.

Q4 2026

Implement mandatory manager training on unconscious bias in performance evaluation, with specific modules addressing the evaluation of employees returning from parental leave. Institute a mandatory calibration review for any “developing” or below-expectations rating assigned to an employee within 12 months of returning from leave of 8+ weeks.

2027

Publish Amazon's first annual Gender Pay Transparency Report, including full Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition, parenthood penalty analysis, and country-by-country comparisons with specific reduction targets. The report will be independently audited and certified by Mercer, and made available to all employees and the public.

The Stakes

The Cost of Inaction

The motherhood penalty is not only a problem of fairness, though it is profoundly that. It is also a problem of economic efficiency. When talented women exit the workforce, stagnate in roles below their demonstrated capability, or reduce their hours because the structural environment makes full participation impossible, the loss is borne simultaneously by families, by companies, and by entire economies.

For Amazon specifically, the cost of inaction is measurable and large. Mercer estimates that maternal attrition alone — the departure of women who leave the company within 24 months of returning from maternity leave — costs Amazon approximately $800 million per year in direct recruiting costs, training and onboarding expenses, lost institutional knowledge, and reduced team productivity during transition periods. Each L6 employee departure represents an estimated $185,000 in total replacement costs. Each L7+ departure costs upward of $400,000. These are costs that are currently invisible in Amazon's financial reporting but are no less real for being uncounted.

The companies that close the motherhood penalty first will have a decisive, compounding advantage in the global competition for talent. Women represent 50% of the global talent pool. Companies that can retain and advance mothers — rather than losing them at the moment of highest training investment — will access deeper talent, generate stronger teams, and build more durable competitive advantages. The question for Amazon is not whether to act. It is how quickly.

Blinder-Oaxaca Decomposition: Full Specification

The Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition separates the mean log-wage difference between two groups (here, men and women) into a component “explained” by group differences in observable productive characteristics and a residual “unexplained” component that captures differences in returns to those characteristics:

\overline{\ln(TC)}_M - \overline{\ln(TC)}_F = \underbrace{(\bar{X}_M - \bar{X}_F)\hat{\beta}_M}_{\text{Explained (Endowments)}} + \underbrace{\bar{X}_F(\hat{\beta}_M - \hat{\beta}_F)}_{\text{Unexplained (Coefficients)}}

The explained component captures differences in endowments — the fact that men and women at Amazon differ in their average job level, tenure, departmental distribution, and educational credentials. The unexplained component captures differences in returns to those endowments — the fact that even men and women with identical observable characteristics are compensated differently. This residual is the most commonly cited measure of potential discrimination, though it may also capture unmeasured productivity differences.

Fair Salary Estimation and Correction

For each female employee, we estimate a “fair” salary by predicting her compensation based on the male coefficient structure applied to her individual characteristics:

\widehat{TC}_{\text{fair},i} = \exp\!\left(\hat{\beta}_0^M + \hat{\beta}_2^M \text{Level}_i + \hat{\beta}_3^M \text{Tenure}_i + \hat{\beta}_4^M \text{Dept}_i + \hat{\beta}_5^M \text{Edu}_i\right)

The recommended correction for each individual is:

\text{Correction}_i = \max\!\left(0,\;\widehat{TC}_{\text{fair},i} - TC_{\text{actual},i}\right)

Compounding Career Penalty Model

The cumulative career penalty is modeled as the product of annual gap ratios over T years:

\text{Gap}_T = 1 - \prod_{t=1}^{T} \frac{1 + r_{\text{base}} + r_{\text{promo}} \cdot P(\text{promo}|\text{parent})}{1 + r_{\text{base}} + r_{\text{promo}} \cdot P(\text{promo}|\text{no child})}

This model demonstrates why even small annual differences in promotion probability compound into large lifetime earnings gaps. A 5 percentage point annual difference in promotion probability (e.g., 12% vs. 17%) produces a 22% cumulative gap over 20 years, consistent with our empirical findings.
