Chapter One

The Child Penalty

In 2019, economist Henrik Kleven and colleagues published what has become one of the most influential studies in labor economics. Tracking millions of workers across Denmark and five other countries, they documented a stark pattern: the birth of a first child triggers an immediate and lasting collapse in women's earnings, while men's earnings are essentially unaffected. They called it the child penalty.

The mechanism is brutally simple. Mothers reduce work hours, switch to more family-friendly (and lower-paying) occupations, and accept fewer promotions. Fathers, on average, do none of these things. The result is a divergence that begins at birth and never fully closes.

The magnitude varies dramatically by country—from roughly 10% in Scandinavia to over 40% in Japan and parts of the developing world. But the direction is universal. Across every economy studied, in every decade of data, the child penalty falls almost entirely on mothers.

What makes the Kleven findings so powerful is their use of event-study methodology with administrative data. Rather than comparing mothers to childless women (which introduces selection bias—women who choose not to have children may differ systematically from those who do), the researchers compare each individual's earnings before and after childbirth. The "treatment" is the birth event itself, and the "control" is the counterfactual trajectory estimated from pre-birth trends. This design eliminates many of the confounds that plagued earlier studies.

The findings also reveal something important about timing. The penalty is immediate—earnings drop within the first year, driven by reduced hours and, in some countries, workforce exit. But the partial recovery that follows is slower than most people assume, and it plateaus well below the pre-birth trajectory. In the United States, mothers' earnings are still 26% below the counterfactual ten years after their first child. In Japan, the gap is 38%. In Denmark—often held up as a model of gender equality—it is still 13%.

The fathers' line tells its own story. In no country do men experience an earnings penalty from having children. In several countries, they experience a small bonus—consistent with sociological research showing that fatherhood is perceived as a signal of stability and commitment, while motherhood is perceived as a signal of divided attention.

Historical Context

How We Came to Understand the Penalty

The motherhood penalty was not always visible. For decades, economists attributed the gender pay gap to differences in education, experience, and occupational choice. It took a generation of research to isolate the specific role of children.

1992
Jane Waldfogel publishes early evidence that mothers earn significantly less than childless women, even after controlling for education and experience. The term "family gap" enters academic vocabulary.
2001
Michelle Budig and Paula England estimate a 7% wage penalty per child for US mothers, one of the first rigorous quantifications. They also document the "fatherhood bonus"—men with children earn more, not less.
2007
Shelley Correll et al. conduct an audit study sending identical resumes with and without signals of motherhood. Mothers are rated as less competent and offered $11,000 lower starting salaries. Fathers are offered $6,000 more than childless men.
2010
Bertrand, Goldin, and Katz track MBA graduates from a top business school. Within 15 years of graduation, mothers earn 60% less than fathers—driven primarily by career interruptions and reduced hours.
2014
Claudia Goldin (later Nobel laureate, 2023) publishes "A Grand Gender Convergence" arguing that the remaining gender gap is driven not by discrimination in hiring but by labor market structures that disproportionately reward long, inflexible hours.
2019
Henrik Kleven et al. publish the landmark cross-country child penalty study, using administrative data from six countries. They show the child penalty explains the vast majority of the remaining gender earnings gap in every country studied.
2022
Andresen and Nix use data from same-sex couples in Norway to show the penalty falls on the birth mother specifically—suggesting biological and social role assignment, not gender per se, drives the effect.
2025
Takeda internal audit applies Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition to its global workforce of 49,000 employees, finding patterns consistent with the academic literature: a 2.9% unexplained gender gap that concentrates among mothers.

Chapter Two

The Catch-Up Myth

Perhaps the most insidious aspect of the motherhood penalty is the widespread belief that it is temporary. "She will catch up once the kids are in school," goes the conventional wisdom. The data tells a different story.

Career earnings are not linear—they compound. A missed promotion at year five does not just cost one year of higher salary; it costs every subsequent raise, bonus, and retirement contribution built on that higher base. A two-year gap in experience does not merely delay advancement by two years; it permanently bends the trajectory downward.

Claudia Goldin, the 2023 Nobel laureate in economics, documented this compounding effect across professions. In finance, law, and medicine, the penalty is steepest—precisely because these fields most heavily reward uninterrupted, long-hours availability. The pharmaceutical industry, with its demanding R&D cycles and global operations, shares many of these characteristics.

The compounding effect explains why the gender pay gap persists even as educational attainment has equalized and overt discrimination has declined. Women do not earn less because they are less qualified. They earn less because the architecture of careers was designed for workers without caregiving responsibilities—which, in practice, means it was designed for men.

The catch-up myth persists because it serves a comforting function. If the penalty is temporary, then no structural change is needed; individual mothers just need to "lean in" harder. But the data is clear: without systemic intervention, the gap compounds into a chasm.

Chapter Three

What Our Own Data Reveals

Takeda Pharmaceuticals employs approximately 49,000 people across more than 80 countries. In 2025, the company conducted its most comprehensive internal pay equity audit to date, applying the Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition method to isolate how much of the observed gender pay gap can be explained by legitimate factors—and how much cannot.

The raw gender pay gap at Takeda is 14.2%. Much of this is explained by differences in job level, tenure, department, and education—the structural factors that reflect historical patterns of who holds which roles. But after controlling for every measurable factor, a 2.9% unexplained gap persists.

That residual is important. It may reflect unmeasured differences in negotiation, unobserved skill depreciation during leave, or outright bias. Whatever its source, it represents real money: for a senior manager earning $150,000, 2.9% is over $4,300 per year—compounding, year after year.

And when we layer in parental status, the picture sharpens dramatically. The penalty concentrates not on women broadly, but specifically on mothers.

The parenthood chart reveals the crux of the problem. Men with children actually earn more than men without children—a phenomenon known as the "fatherhood bonus," documented by sociologist Michelle Budig and others. Meanwhile, women with children earn dramatically less than any other group. The gap between fathers and mothers is not 14%—it is 21 points on the index.

This asymmetry is critical for understanding why the gender pay gap has proven so resistant to closing. If the gap were driven by gender alone, it would affect all women roughly equally. Instead, it concentrates like a laser on mothers. Childless women at Takeda earn 94% of what childless men earn—a 6% gap that is largely explained by job level and departmental distribution. But mothers earn only 81% of what childless men earn, and only 79% of what fathers earn. Parenthood does not just reveal a gap; it creates one.

The leave uptake data shown above underscores the mechanism. In countries where men take substantial parental leave—Sweden, Denmark—the motherhood penalty is smallest. In countries where men take almost no leave—Japan, India—it is largest. The correlation is not coincidental. When caregiving is shared, career disruption is shared. When it is not, women bear the cost alone.

Chapter Four

A Global Problem, Local Shapes

The motherhood penalty exists everywhere, but its severity and mechanisms vary enormously. National policy, cultural norms, and childcare infrastructure interact to produce very different outcomes for working mothers. The cross-country variation is itself informative: it proves that the penalty is not biologically determined but socially constructed.

If biology were destiny, the penalty would be roughly equal everywhere. Instead, it ranges from approximately 10% in Sweden to over 50% in parts of South Asia. The difference is policy. The difference is infrastructure. The difference is whether a society treats childcare as a private burden or a public investment.

For Takeda, which operates manufacturing plants in Hikari and Osaka, research centers in Shonan and Cambridge, commercial offices in Zurich and Sao Paulo, and corporate functions in Tokyo and Boston, these regional variations are not abstract. They directly shape the career trajectories, compensation levels, and retention patterns of thousands of employees. Five regional profiles illustrate the range—and the lessons.

The Nordic Model: Denmark and Sweden

The Nordic countries represent the global gold standard for family policy, with decades of investment in shared parental leave and universal childcare. Their experience shows what is possible—and what remains stubbornly difficult to change.

  • Shared parental leave with use-it-or-lose-it father quotas (90 days reserved for each parent in Sweden; similar quotas in Iceland, Norway, and Denmark)
  • Universal subsidized childcare from age 1; fees capped at approximately 3% of household income in Sweden, with guaranteed placement within 3 months of application
  • High female labor force participation rates (76-80%), among the highest globally
  • Strong cultural norm of dual-earner households; fatherhood leave is socially expected and taking it carries no professional stigma
  • Generous but time-limited income replacement (approximately 80% of salary) designed to encourage return to work rather than extended absence
  • Public sector employment disproportionately female, contributing to occupational segregation that partially offsets policy gains
Result: Smallest motherhood penalty among studied countries (approximately 10-15% long-run earnings loss). But even here, the gap persists. Women remain underrepresented in top management, C-suite positions, and corporate boards despite decades of progressive policy. The "glass ceiling" and occupational segregation between male-dominated private sector and female-dominated public sector continue to generate pay inequality at the top of the distribution.

United States: Market-Dependent Parenthood

The United States stands alone among wealthy nations in its lack of federal paid leave. The result is a patchwork system where a mother's economic outcome depends heavily on her employer, her state, and her income level.

  • No federal paid family leave mandate; the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) provides 12 weeks unpaid leave but only for workers at firms with 50+ employees who have worked 1,250+ hours—excluding approximately 40% of the workforce
  • Employer-dependent benefits create a two-tier system: technology and finance workers may receive 16-20 weeks fully paid; service, retail, and hourly workers often receive zero paid days
  • Average childcare cost: approximately $15,000 per year per child nationally, rising to $20,000-30,000 in major metropolitan areas—exceeding in-state university tuition in 28 states
  • Absence of universal pre-K; public school schedules (typically ending at 2:30-3:00pm) are misaligned with standard work hours, creating a daily logistical crisis for working parents
  • Strong professional stigma around career gaps; resume "black holes" during childrearing years significantly reduce callback rates in experimental studies
  • State-level variation is significant: California, New York, New Jersey, and Washington offer paid family leave programs; the majority of states offer nothing beyond federal FMLA
Result: One of the largest motherhood penalties among developed nations (approximately 30-40% long-run earnings loss). The penalty is highly regressive: low-income mothers are hit hardest, often forced out of the workforce entirely when childcare costs exceed their take-home pay. The US remains the only OECD nation—and one of only six countries worldwide—without guaranteed paid maternity leave at the national level.

Japan: Generous Policy, Rigid Culture

Japan illustrates a paradox: progressive policy on paper, regressive outcomes in practice. The gap between statutory entitlements and cultural reality is wider here than in any other OECD country.

  • Generous statutory maternity and paternity leave (up to one year at approximately 67% pay replacement)
  • Cultural reality: only approximately 14% of eligible fathers take any paternity leave; average duration among those who do is 5 days
  • "Matahara" (maternity harassment) still widely reported—social and workplace pressure on women to quit after childbirth
  • Seniority-based pay and promotion systems amplify the cost of any career interruption exponentially
  • Limited availability of quality childcare, especially in urban centers; approximately 23,000 children on waitlists nationally
Result: Very high motherhood penalty (approximately 40-50% long-run loss). Japan's M-shaped female labor force participation curve—high entry, sharp drop at childbearing age, partial recovery in lower-paying non-regular roles—remains one of the most dramatic in the OECD despite reform efforts.

Germany: The Long Leave Trap

Germany's experience reveals an uncomfortable truth: generous parental leave can backfire if it is not designed to encourage gender-equal uptake. Extended leave periods, combined with tax and cultural incentives for one parent to stay home, have created a "long leave trap" that disproportionately affects women.

  • Up to 3 years of job-protected parental leave per child; Elterngeld (parental allowance) at 65-67% of net income for 12-14 months
  • Half-day school system historically designed around the assumption of a stay-at-home parent
  • "Minijob" culture: many mothers return to marginal, low-paying part-time positions (520 euros per month tax-free threshold)
  • Joint taxation system (Ehegattensplitting) creates a strong financial disincentive for the lower-earning spouse—overwhelmingly the mother—to work more hours
  • Cultural concept of "Rabenmutter" (raven mother)—social stigma against mothers who work full-time or return to work "too early"
Result: Long leave periods correlate with longer career gaps and deeper skill depreciation (approximately 25-35% long-run loss). Germany's generous leave policy paradoxically reinforces traditional gender roles by making it economically rational for one parent—almost always the mother—to remain home for extended periods.

Emerging Economies: India and Brazil

In the world's two largest developing economies, the motherhood penalty interacts with deep structural inequalities in ways that can be devastating. The formal-informal sector divide means that labor protections exist on paper but reach only a fraction of working women.

  • Formal sector workers have some statutory protections (India: 26 weeks paid maternity leave since 2017; Brazil: 120 days, extendable to 180)
  • Informal sector workers—representing 40-90% of the female workforce in many regions—receive zero protections or benefits
  • Severe childcare infrastructure gaps, especially in rural areas and smaller cities; formal childcare often unavailable or unaffordable
  • Heavy reliance on extended family (grandmothers in particular) as the primary childcare solution
  • Cultural expectations around motherhood remain more rigid; domestic labor is heavily gendered with limited male participation
  • India: female labor force participation dropped from 32% to 24% between 2005 and 2019 before partially recovering post-pandemic
Result: Severe penalty, often amounting to complete and permanent workforce exit. In India, over 70% of women who leave formal employment for childcare never return. In Brazil, the informal-to-formal sector gap means many mothers cycle into lower-paying, unprotected work with no path back to their pre-child career trajectory.
Chapter Five

What Would Actually Work

The research literature is unambiguous on one point: the motherhood penalty is not inevitable. It is the product of policy choices, institutional design, and cultural norms—all of which can be changed. The Nordic countries have demonstrated that aggressive policy intervention can reduce the penalty by 60-70% compared to countries like the US and Japan. But even the best-performing nations have not eliminated it entirely, suggesting that policy alone is insufficient without concurrent changes in workplace culture and social expectations.

For a global employer like Takeda, the challenge is both structural and cultural. A company operating across 80+ countries cannot rely on any single national policy framework. It must build internal standards that exceed local minimums while respecting local contexts. The following six evidence-based recommendations are drawn from the academic literature, best-practice case studies, and Takeda's own internal analysis.

  1. 1

    Universal Paid Parental Leave (12+ weeks, shared)

    Use-it-or-lose-it father quotas are the single most effective policy lever. Countries that reserve non-transferable leave for fathers see dramatically higher male uptake and more equitable division of childcare labor. Takeda should mandate a minimum of 12 weeks fully paid leave for all parents globally, regardless of local statutory minimums.

  2. 2

    Subsidized Childcare (cap at 7% of household income)

    When childcare costs exceed a mother's take-home pay, the economic calculus pushes her out of the workforce. Government subsidies or employer-sponsored backup care programs can break this trap. Nordic evidence demonstrates a return on investment of 2-3x in increased tax revenue and economic output from higher female labor force participation.

  3. 3

    Mandatory Pay Transparency

    Publish gender and parental-status pay gap reports annually. The EU Pay Transparency Directive (2023) provides a regulatory model. Research consistently shows that companies required to report gaps are 10-15% more likely to close them within five years compared to voluntary disclosure regimes.

  4. 4

    Returnship Programs

    Structured re-entry programs for workers returning from career breaks of one year or more. Effective returnships include dedicated mentorship, technical skills refreshers, and fast-track performance evaluation. Goldman Sachs, J.P. Morgan, and several pharmaceutical companies have piloted successful models with retention rates exceeding 80%.

  5. 5

    Flexible Work Without Career Penalty

    Part-time and remote work arrangements must not become career dead-ends. Organizations should explicitly decouple flexibility from promotion eligibility, track promotion rates by work arrangement, publish the data internally, and normalize flexibility for all genders—not just mothers.

  6. 6

    Corporate Accountability

    Tie executive compensation to measurable equity KPIs: adjusted pay gap closure, promotion parity by gender and parental status, and parental leave uptake rates among men. What gets measured and incentivized gets done. Decades of evidence show that voluntary commitments alone are insufficient to drive structural change.

The Broader Picture

The Cost of Inaction

The motherhood penalty is not just a fairness problem. It is an economic problem. McKinsey Global Institute has estimated that closing the gender gap in labor force participation could add $12 trillion to global GDP by 2025. The International Monetary Fund has found that reducing gender inequality in the labor market is associated with higher economic growth, greater financial stability, and lower income inequality.

For individual companies, the business case is equally compelling. High-performing women who leave after childbirth represent lost recruitment, training, and institutional knowledge investments. Deloitte estimates the cost of replacing a mid-career professional at 100-150% of annual salary. When a pharmaceutical company loses a senior scientist or experienced commercial manager to the motherhood penalty, it is not just losing a worker—it is losing years of accumulated expertise in drug development, regulatory knowledge, or market relationships.

Takeda's own internal data suggests that female attrition rates spike by 34% in the two years following a first child, compared to baseline turnover. Among women in technical and scientific roles, the spike is even higher: 41%. These are not junior employees; the average tenure of women who leave after childbirth is 6.8 years.

The calculus is simple. Every mother who leaves the workforce or downshifts her career because of inadequate support represents a triple loss: to the individual (in earnings, professional fulfillment, and retirement security), to the employer (in talent, expertise, and replacement costs), and to the economy (in reduced output, tax revenue, and human capital utilization).

The question is not whether companies can afford to address the motherhood penalty. The question is whether they can afford not to.

Flexibility Audit: Takeda will conduct its first-ever analysis of promotion rates by work arrangement (full-time vs. part-time vs. remote), with results published internally. Any statistically significant disparities will trigger corrective action plans within 90 days.

These commitments represent meaningful steps, but Takeda acknowledges they are the beginning of a long-term effort, not the end. The motherhood penalty was not created overnight and will not be eliminated overnight. Sustained attention, rigorous measurement, and institutional willpower are required.

Technical Appendix and Methodology ▼

Blinder-Oaxaca Decomposition

The Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition separates the mean outcome difference between two groups into a component explained by group differences in observable characteristics (the "explained" portion) and a residual component (the "unexplained" portion, which may reflect discrimination, omitted variable bias, or unobserved heterogeneity).

ln(W̄M) - ln(W̄F) = (X̄M - X̄F)β̂M + X̄F(β̂M - β̂F)

Where:
  ln(W̄) = mean log total compensation
  X̄ = vector of mean covariate values (job level, tenure, education, department, country)
  β̂ = OLS coefficient estimates from gender-specific log-wage regressions
  First term = "explained" component (differences in endowments/characteristics)
  Second term = "unexplained" component (differences in returns to characteristics)

Regression Specification

ln(TotalCompi) = α + β1Femalei + β2Parenti + β3(Female × Parent)i
  + γ1JobLeveli + γ2Tenurei + γ3Tenure2i + γ4Educationi
  + δjDepartmentj + φkCountryk + εi

Key coefficients:
  β1 = baseline gender effect (female penalty, holding all else equal)
  β2 = parenthood effect (common to both genders)
  β3 = interaction: the additional penalty of being female AND a parent
      (i.e., the motherhood penalty net of the baseline gender effect and fatherhood effect)

Data Extraction

Cleaning and Harmonization
Compensation converted to USD using 12-month average exchange rates. Job levels harmonized across country-specific grading systems to Takeda global bands (L1-L12).

OLS regression on log total compensation with full covariate set. Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition estimated using Neumark (1988) pooled specification as robustness check.

Validation and Reporting
Results validated against prior-year audit. Sensitivity analysis conducted on model specification. Regional breakdowns computed for countries with n > 200 employees.

Limitations

  • Internal Takeda compensation data presented in this report is synthetic and generated for illustrative purposes only. Actual internal audit results are confidential and proprietary.
  • The Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition fundamentally cannot distinguish between discrimination and unobserved productivity differences (e.g., skill depreciation during extended leave, differences in work intensity preferences). The "unexplained" component is an upper bound on potential discrimination.
  • Cross-country pay gap comparisons are complicated by differences in compensation structures (base salary vs. bonus vs. equity), cost of living, purchasing power parity, and local reporting conventions.
  • Personal stories presented in this report are composites drawn from published accounts, anonymized interviews, and publicly reported experiences. Names and identifying details have been changed to protect individual privacy.
  • The career path simulator uses deliberately simplified assumptions about raise rates, promotion probabilities, and leave durations. Real career trajectories are substantially more heterogeneous and influenced by many factors beyond parental status.
  • Child penalty estimates for India and Brazil are based on smaller and less nationally representative datasets than those available for Denmark, the UK, and other OECD countries. These estimates should be treated as indicative rather than precise.
